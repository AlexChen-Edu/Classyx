If Your Kid Sits at That Desk for an Hour and Still Gets C's, Read This

The study time myth nobody talks about

If your kid spends an hour on homework every night and you're still getting emails from teachers about missing assignments, the problem isn't effort. It's what they're actually doing with that hour.

Most kids aren't lazy. They're just studying wrong.

There's a huge difference between sitting at a desk and actually retaining information. Your kid can have their textbook open, their notes out, their laptop on — and still absorb almost nothing. It happens all the time and parents have no idea because from the outside it looks exactly like studying.

The research on this is pretty clear. Re-reading notes is one of the least effective ways to study. Highlighting is even worse. Kids feel productive doing it because they're doing something, but very little of it actually sticks.

What actually works is active recall — being forced to retrieve information from memory rather than just looking at it. Flashcards. Practice problems. Explaining something out loud without looking at your notes. Anything that makes your brain work to find the answer rather than just recognize it.

The problem is that kind of studying is harder and less comfortable, so most kids avoid it without even realizing that's what they're doing.

How long should they actually be studying?

A rough guide that most teachers and counselors agree on: 10 minutes per grade level per night. So a 7th grader should be doing about 70 minutes of focused work. A 10th grader around 100 minutes.

But that's focused work — not time spent in the general vicinity of a textbook.

If your kid is hitting those numbers and still struggling, the issue is probably comprehension, not time. They're lost on the material and going through the motions because they don't know what else to do.

What to look for

Ask your kid to explain what they studied tonight without looking at anything. Not summarize — explain it to you like you don't know it. If they can't do it, they don't know it yet. That's your signal.
